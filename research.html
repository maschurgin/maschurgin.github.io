My research program is focused on determining the nature of representations underlying visual cognition and memory. My work is guided by the premise that memory is inherently interactive with and potentially dependent on other cognitive systems (e.g., attention, perception, learning, etc). To probe the underlying structure and content of visual memory, ​I use a wide range of methods, including behavioral measures, eye-tracking, computational modeling, Bayesian statistics, and electrophysiology (EEG). My research has focused on three core areas:

  1. How is visual memory shaped by constraints on perception?
  2. What are the roles and underlying representations involved in visual working memory and visual long-term memory?
  3. How does memory change over the course of experience?

​Visual Memory is Shaped by Constraints on Perception ​

Construction of visual memory must rely on perceptual mechanisms to facilitate the integration of information into unified object representations. However, the way perception shapes our memory representations is understudied. In my work, I ask what features of perception are in place to support the formation of robust memory representations. I have found that object kinematics assist in the construction of tolerant long-term memories, that unbiased working memories can produce biased responses through non-uniformities in attention, and that taking into account how we perceive stimuli allows for a simple yet powerful unified framework of visual memory.

The Roles of Working Memory and Long-Term Memory

The distinction between visual working and long-term memory is among the most entrenched in research on human cognition. While these systems are typically compared and contrasted, it has remained largely ignored how these systems may directly support one another. I have discovered that features of working memory are actually in place to support the acquisition of robust long-term memories, and that the episodic long-term memory for an item can replace active maintenance for that same item in working memory.​

How does Memory change over the course of experience?

A person sees an object once, and then seconds, minutes, hours, days, or weeks later, she sees it again. How is the person’s visual memory for that object changed, improved, or degraded by the second encounter, compared to a situation in which she will have only seen the object once? The overwhelming majority of research considers recognition following a single exposure to a set of objects, whereas objects reoccur regularly in lived experience. I have discovered several factors, such as the quality of encounters or the amount of time between encounters, that further our understanding how memory changes, improves, and degrades over the course of experience.
